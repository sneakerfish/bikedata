A Tale of Three Cities: Examining Bike-share Growth in the Bay, Boston, and New York City Metropolitan areas from 2017 to the Present

Introduction: Bike Share Systems by the Numbers

There has been many studies and analysis on individual bike-share systems but not many visualizations that compare these systems side-by-side in a visual way. Our team's goal is to present to you our findings and a tool that compares three of the biggest bike-share systems in the United States.

The Bay, Boston, and New York City metropolitan areas established their bike-share programs in the early 2010's.

While each city offers similar membership plans and pricing, New York City's Citibike is the most expensive followed by the Bay Area's Bay Wheels. Meanwhile, Boston's Blue Bikes is significantly more affordable at almost 40% cheaper than New York City's annual plan.

New York City's system is signifcantly more extensive in terms of number of stations when compared to the other programs but supports a much larger population.

Bike-share System Trends Across Time

In terms of cumulative round trips, New York City due to its population has a larger number of cumulative trips since 2017.

Bay Area

Unchecking Boston Metro and New York City Metro and viewing only the Bay Area data, we see that the Bay Area's ridership stayed relatively constant since 2017. The system started to grow in terms of number of trips in 2020 but stopped due to COVID19 pandemic. Ridership still hasn't recovered. We suspect it's due to major tech companies such as Apple, Facebook, and Google continuing to support work-from-home policies until at least January 2022. Boston Metro

Uncheck Bay Area and New York City Metro and viewing only the Boston Metro data, we see that Blue Bikes experienced continued steady growth since 2017 with a drop during the start of the COVID19 pandemic. Unlike San Francisco, Boston's ridership in terms of trip appears to have recovered above pre-COVID19 levels. Please hover the large circles to view interesting events.

New York City Metro

Uncheck Bay Area and Boston Metro and viewing only the New York City Metro data, we observe that New York City follows a similar pattern to Boston in terms of ridership growth. Please hover the large circles to view interesting events.

General Trends

Many of the steep declines in the number of trips appear to be happen over holidays or on a weekend. We suspect that many people use bike-share systems primarily as a mode of transportation for work rather than pleasure

Trip Duration

Checking all the checkbox's for each reason, we can see clearly that riders in Boston typically travel longer on their trips when compared to riders of other systems.

Using the slider and region area buttons, we can examine movement across cities on a typical day. We see that in the morning of each region, that riders are leaving their homes and heading towards the city center and vice-versa during the vening.

Examining this radar polar coordinate plot that shows ridership across time, we see that the number of riders increase during business hours and decreases in the late evening.

Concluding Statements

We can see that bike-share ridership has steadily grown since 2017. We also expect that as companies begin to recall their workers back to the office that ridership will continue to grow and this should be the most pronounced in San Francisco.

It is also our observation that the purpose of many of these rides is for commuting to work supported by our analysis of which stations trips are being are made from and the days and times in which these trips are being made.
