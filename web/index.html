Bike-share usage in the San Francisco, Boston, and New York City metropolitan areas

Introduction

Using data from three of the largest bike-share networks in the United States, this website empowers users to answer the question: "What influences bike-share usage?"

Comparing Bike-shares

Each city offers similar membership plans, but Boston's Blue Bikes is more affordable New York City's annual plan.

New York City's system is significantly more larger and more extensive with more than twice the number of stations as Boston.

Bike-share System Trends Across Time

New York City, unsurprisingly, has had the largest number of bike share trips since 2017.

The number of stations in each city has gradually increased since 2017.

Bay Area

The Bay Area's ridership grew unevenly until the beginning of 2020 when ridership boomed.

However, ridership plummeted due to COVID-19 pandemic and has not fully recovered since.

This could be because major employers like Apple, Facebook, and Google are extending their work-from-home policies until at least January 2022.

Boston Metro

Boston's ridership grew steadily with a drop during the start of the COVID-19 pandemic.

But unlike the Bay Area, Boston's bikeshare usage has recovered to higher than pre-COVID-19 levels.

NYC Metro

Ridership in the NYC Metro follows a similar pattern to Boston in terms of ridership growth.

Unlike the Bay Area, major Wall St employers have been actively encouraging workers to come back to the office

Moreover, NYC's mayor has ordered government employees back to the office on a full-time schedule.

General Trends

There are steep declines in the number of trips occurring during holidays or on weekends.

Leading us to conclude that many people use bike-share systems primarily for their work-commute rather than pleasure

General Trends

Despite colder winters, Boston metro riders typically take longer rides (measured in minutes) than their New York and San Francisco counterparts.

All metros experienced a period of sustained longer median rides during the first wave of COVID-19 in 2020.

Seasonality

In Boston and New York City, cyclists take longer rides as the weather gets warmer.

In contrast, San Francisco does not experience the same seasonal increases in ridership.
